CSF Tau–Amyloid Imbalance Emerges Earliest in Preclinical Alzheimer’s Disease: A 
Multimodal Timing Analysis in ADNI 
Russell Butler* 
*Bishops University, Department of Computer Science, Sherbrooke, Quebec J1M 1Z7 
Abstract 
Background 
Early detection of Alzheimer's disease involves at least two related transition stages: movement from 
normal cognition into impairment and progression from mild cognitive impairment (MCI) to Alzheimer's 
disease (AD) dementia. Invasive markers such as cerebrospinal fluid (CSF) assays and positron emission 
tomography (PET) may capture earlier biology, while non-invasive markers such as plasma assays, 
magnetic resonance imaging (MRI), and cognition may be easier to scale. 
Objective 
To characterize biomarker timing for the earliest clinical transition detectable in this cohort, compare 
predictive performance across baseline CN to MCI/AD and baseline MCI to AD conversion tasks, and test 
long-horizon prediction of eventual AD among subjects starting from normal cognition. 
Methods 
We built a reproducible analysis pipeline using ADNI diagnosis, demographics, cognition, genotype, MRI, 
amyloid PET, tau PET, CSF , and plasma tables. The base longitudinal cohort contained 3,788 subjects 
and 15,879 visits. Conversion status was assigned from longitudinal diagnosis trajectories. The 
longitudinal timing analysis focused on 1,601 baseline cognitively normal participants, including 194 
progressors and 1,407 stable controls, because direct CN to AD events were uncommon. For each 
biomarker, yearly pre-conversion bins were compared with stable control follow-up bins after collapsing 
repeated observations within each subject-year bin. We then evaluated baseline prediction within 4 years 
for both CN to MCI/AD and MCI to AD, compared three 7-year baseline models in both baseline CN and 
baseline MCI participants, and performed a third long-horizon analysis restricted to baseline CN subjects 
who later reached AD either directly or through an intermediate MCI stage. 
Results 
After subject-level binning, the earliest detectable divergence in the CN-based timing analysis was the 
CSF pTau/A beta 42 ratio at 7 years before conversion, followed by CSF A beta 42 at 7 years, APOE4 count 
at 6 years, and MRI hippocampal volume, amyloid PET measures, and plasma GFAP at 5 years. In the 
FDR-corrected summary, CSF A beta 42 remained significant at 7 years, while the CSF pTau/A beta 42 
ratio remained significant at 4 years. For 4-year prediction, random forest models reached AUC 0.846 for 
baseline cognitively normal participants progressing to MCI or AD and AUC 0.893 for baseline MCI 
participants progressing to AD. For 7-year prediction, the all baseline numeric model performed best in 
both stages, reaching AUC 0.885 in baseline CN participants and AUC 0.911 in baseline MCI participants. 
In the long-horizon baseline CN to AD analysis, 46 subjects eventually reached AD and 39 did so within 
12 years; the strongest biomarker panel achieved AUC 0.926 and the all baseline numeric model 
achieved AUC 0.940.

Conclusions 
In this ADNI analysis, invasive markers retained the earliest temporal signal for transition out of normal 
cognition, with CSF abnormalities preceding most non-invasive measures. Across both CN and MCI 
baseline states, multimodal prediction performed well, with particularly strong performance for MCI to 
AD progression. A separate long-horizon baseline CN to AD analysis suggested that blood and CSF 
biomarkers can carry substantial signal even 10 to 12 years before first AD diagnosis, although the event 
count was small. The overall pattern supports a staged framework in which invasive markers define the 
earliest biological window, while broader multimodal models support practical risk stratification across 
later clinical transition stages. 
Introduction 
Alzheimer's disease evolves over many years before a formal diagnosis changes, and several influential 
biomarker models have argued that the underlying pathophysiological process begins long before overt 
clinical dementia [1-7]. A useful early-detection framework therefore has to address at least two distinct 
but connected transition problems: which biomarkers change first before a cognitively normal person 
becomes clinically impaired, and which markers best predict progression once impairment is already 
present. 
These are related but not identical questions. A marker can be biologically early yet less useful for short-
horizon prediction, while a later marker can still be clinically valuable if it is easier to deploy and strongly 
associated with near-term progression. This distinction is especially relevant when comparing invasive 
and non-invasive modalities [1-3,6-8]. CSF assays and PET imaging provide relatively direct access to 
molecular pathology but require lumbar puncture or advanced imaging infrastructure. Plasma 
biomarkers, MRI, and cognitive testing are less invasive and easier to scale, but they may reflect 
downstream pathology or clinical expression rather than the earliest disease state [8-20]. 
Recent studies have shown that plasma p-tau217, plasma Aβ42/Aβ40, and plasma GFAP can identify 
preclinical AD-related change and forecast future cognitive decline, in some cases over long horizons, 
while multimodal models that combine molecular, imaging, genetic, and cognitive features have 
improved prediction of MCI-to-AD progression [8-25]. At the same time, the relative ranking of blood, CSF , 
and PET measures remains unsettled. Several recent head-to-head studies suggest that plasma p-
tau217 or combined blood-marker strategies can approach CSF- or PET-level performance for selected 
outcomes, whereas other studies continue to show stronger performance for amyloid PET or highlight 
marked cohort dependence [26-33]. 
ADNI offers a strong setting for this question because it includes repeated diagnoses, cognitive testing, 
MRI, PET, CSF , plasma, and genotype data collected over years. The challenge is that these data are 
distributed across many tables with heterogeneous visit timing and varying coverage. We addressed this 
by building a constrained, reproducible pipeline focused on a defined set of core files and representative 
biomarkers. 
The paper addresses four related aims: 
1. Identify the earliest detectable biomarker changes preceding the first clinical transition from 
normal cognition to MCI or AD. 

===== PAGE 3 =====
2. Evaluate baseline prediction for both CN to MCI/AD and MCI to AD conversion. 
3. Compare how invasive and non-invasive markers contribute across early transition timing and 
later-stage progression prediction. 
4. Test whether baseline CN subjects who ultimately reach AD can be predicted 10 to 12 years in 
advance using the same multimodal modeling framework. 
Methods 
Study design and data sources 
The analysis used ADNI tables that had already been converted to CSV and indexed in the project dataset 
map. Core clinical tables included diagnosis, demographics, cognition, and APOE genotype. Biomarker 
tables included MRI, amyloid PET, tau PET, CSF , and plasma. A separate extended prediction analysis 
added DWI, vital signs, and composite memory and executive function summaries. 
The core longitudinal tables were: 
DXSUM.csv for diagnosis 
PTDEMOG.csv for demographics 
CDR.csv, MMSE.csv, and MOCA.csv for cognition 
APOERES.csv for genotype 
The biomarker tables were: 
UCSFFSX7.csv for structural MRI 
UCBERKELEY_AMY_6MM.csv for amyloid PET 
UCBERKELEY_TAU_6MM.csv for tau PET 
UPENNBIOMK_ROCHE_ELECSYS.csv for CSF markers 
UPENN_PLASMA_FUJIREBIO_QUANTERIX.csv for plasma markers 
The extended 7-year comparison additionally used:

UWNPSYCHSUM.csv 
NEUROBAT.csv 
Cohort construction 
Visit records were harmonized by subject identifier and visit key. Visit keys were built from VISCODE2 
when available, otherwise VISCODE, and otherwise a standardized date key. Within each subject-visit 
combination, duplicates were collapsed conservatively to a single row. The resulting base longitudinal 
cohort contained 3,788 subjects and 15,879 visits. 
Diagnosis trajectories were sorted chronologically for each subject. Baseline diagnosis was the first non-
missing diagnosis. Conversion was defined as the first transition from cognitively normal to MCI or AD, or 

===== PAGE 4 =====
from MCI to AD. Subjects without a qualifying later transition were treated as stable within their baseline 
group. 
The resulting conversion table classified 3,777 subjects with usable longitudinal diagnosis information: 
1,601 baseline cognitively normal 
1,607 baseline MCI 
569 baseline AD 
1,407 stable cognitively normal 
185 cognitively normal to MCI conversions 
9 cognitively normal to AD conversions 
404 MCI to AD conversions

Alignment and missing data 
Modalities were merged into the clinical cohort using exact visit keys first and nearest-date matching 
within 365 days second. This fallback was necessary because biomarker acquisitions were not 
synchronized across modalities. Coverage was uneven. In the analysis-ready dataset, missingness was 

===== PAGE 5 =====
approximately 18.0 percent for MRI hippocampal volume, 45.4 percent for amyloid PET SUVR, 58.9 
percent for CSF A beta 42, 77.3 percent for tau PET, and 80.4 percent for plasma pTau217. 
Longitudinal timing analysis 
The longitudinal timing analysis was performed for baseline cognitively normal progressors versus stable 
cognitively normal controls. Time zero was defined as the first conversion diagnosis. Each visit was 
aligned to years before conversion, and stable cognitively normal participants were aligned by follow-up 
time from baseline. To avoid inflating significance from repeated visits, repeated measurements within 
each subject-year bin were collapsed to a subject-level median before testing. 
For each biomarker and each yearly bin from 0 to 8 years before conversion, progressors were compared 
with stable controls using Welch's t test. A biomarker was considered detectably divergent when both 
conditions were met: 
p < 0.05 
absolute Cohen's d >= 0.3 
We also summarized FDR-corrected significant bins to distinguish nominal earliest divergence from the 
more conservative multiplicity-adjusted result. 
Baseline prediction analyses 
Two 4-year baseline prediction tasks were evaluated: 
cognitively normal to MCI or AD within 4 years 
MCI to AD within 4 years 
Models included logistic regression, regularized logistic regression, random forest, and gradient boosting. 
Performance was assessed with cross-validated AUC. 
We then performed separate 7-year prediction comparisons in baseline cognitively normal and baseline 
MCI participants. Three random forest feature groups were evaluated in each stage: 
1. strongest biomarkers 
2. MRI plus DWI plus memory plus vital signs 
3. all baseline numeric predictors 
These models used 5-fold cross-validated AUC. Feature importance was estimated with cross-validated 
permutation importance on held-out folds rather than in-sample importance. 
Finally, we performed a third long-horizon analysis restricted to baseline CN subjects who eventually 
reached AD, either through direct CN to AD conversion or via an intermediate MCI stage. The primary 
endpoint was first AD diagnosis within 12 years of baseline, chosen because 39 baseline CN subjects 
met that threshold and 46 eventually reached AD at any observed horizon. 
Results 
Cohort assembly and clinical transition structure 

===== PAGE 6 =====
The assembled cohort supported both longitudinal timing analysis and baseline prediction. Among the 
1,601 baseline cognitively normal participants, 194 eventually progressed and 1,407 remained stable. 
Most progression from normal cognition was to MCI rather than directly to AD, so the timing analysis 
should be interpreted primarily as preclinical to prodromal transition timing rather than pure preclinical 
to dementia timing. 
Among baseline MCI participants, 404 progressed to AD and 1,203 remained stable MCI during observed 
follow-up. This larger event count made the MCI to AD prediction task less sparse than the cognitively 
normal to MCI or AD task and makes the paper more naturally a two-stage transition study than a single-
stage early-detection study. 
Longitudinal timing of markers before transition out of normal cognition 
The CSF pTau/A beta 42 ratio was the earliest marker to meet the nominal divergence threshold, 
appearing 7 years before conversion, with CSF A beta 42 also diverging at 7 years. APOE4 count reached 
divergence at 6 years. MRI hippocampal atrophy, amyloid PET SUVR, amyloid PET centiloid, and plasma 
GFAP all showed divergence at 5 years. Tau PET and plasma pTau217 appeared at 4 years, while CDR-SB 
emerged at 3 years. 
This ordering suggests a layered sequence that is broadly consistent with prior biomarker-cascade 
models but also with more recent work emphasizing early CSF tau-amyloid imbalance, early CSF amyloid 
change, and later emergence of more scalable plasma and structural markers [1-7,15-19]. The earliest 
detectable changes in this dataset were invasive molecular markers, especially CSF tau-amyloid 
imbalance. A second phase included structural neurodegeneration and selected non-invasive blood-
based or imaging-derived markers. Cognitive decline became visible later, closer to overt diagnostic 
transition. 
The top portion of the nominal divergence ranking was: 
1. CSF pTau/A beta 42 ratio at 7 years 
2. CSF A beta 42 at 7 years 
3. APOE4 count at 6 years 
4. MRI normalized hippocampal volume at 5 years 
5. Amyloid PET SUVR at 5 years 
6. Amyloid PET centiloid at 5 years 
7. Plasma GFAP at 5 years 
8. MOCA at 4 years 
9. Tau PET meta-temporal SUVR at 4 years 
10. Plasma pTau217 at 4 years 
In the FDR-based summary, CSF A beta 42 remained at 7 years, while CSF pTau/A beta 42 ratio, plasma 
pTau217, and MOCA remained significant at 4 years. This more conservative view still supports an early 

===== PAGE 7 =====
invasive signal, but it should temper any overly rigid claims about the exact top ordering. Corresponding 
CN and MCI trajectory panels and divergence timelines are included in the figure set for visual stage 
comparison. 
Baseline prediction across both clinical transition stages 
Short-horizon prediction told a somewhat different story from longitudinal timing. For baseline 
cognitively normal participants, the best 4-year model was a random forest with AUC 0.846 for 
progression to MCI or AD. For baseline MCI participants, the best model was again random forest with 
AUC 0.893 for progression to AD. 
These 4-year results imply that later-stage clinical and neurodegenerative signals can be highly predictive 
over shorter horizons even if they are not the earliest to emerge biologically, a distinction also 
emphasized in prior multimodal prediction studies [20-25]. This is important because the clinically useful 
marker is not always the earliest marker. In practice, a marker that changes somewhat later may still be 
preferable if it improves prediction in the time window that matters for intervention or trial enrollment. 
Single-feature results also differed by stage. In baseline CN participants, MOCA, normalized 
hippocampal volume, and plasma neurofilament light were among the strongest single predictors. In 
baseline MCI participants, CDR-SB and normalized hippocampal volume were particularly strong, 
consistent with more overt clinical and neurodegenerative expression later in disease progression. 
Seven-year baseline prediction in baseline CN participants 
The 7-year baseline comparison focused on baseline cognitively normal participants and directly 
contrasted invasive-heavy and non-invasive-heavy feature sets. There were 1,601 baseline cognitively 
normal participants and 146 conversion events within 7 years. 
The strongest biomarker panel achieved AUC 0.836. The MRI plus DWI plus memory plus vital-sign panel 
achieved AUC 0.799. The broadest model, using all baseline numeric predictors, achieved AUC 0.885. 
The strongest biomarker model was driven mainly by plasma pTau217, plasma A beta 42/A beta 40, 
plasma GFAP , plasma neurofilament light, and tau PET. The MRI plus DWI plus memory plus vitals model 
was led by ADNI memory score and normalized hippocampal volume. In the all-baseline model, the top 
contributors were plasma pTau217, ADNI memory, MOCA, plasma GFAP , hippocampal volume, plasma A 
beta 42/A beta 40, and executive function measures. 
These results sharpen the invasive versus non-invasive interpretation. Purely or predominantly non-
invasive panels remained informative and came close to the strongest biomarker panel, but the best 
overall prediction came from the broad integrated baseline model rather than from a narrower invasive-
only or non-invasive-only subset. 
Seven-year baseline prediction in baseline MCI participants 
The parallel 7-year comparison in baseline MCI participants used the same three feature groups and 
included 1,607 subjects with 370 conversions to AD within 7 years. 

===== PAGE 8 =====
The strongest biomarker panel achieved AUC 0.802. The MRI plus DWI plus memory plus vital-sign panel 
achieved AUC 0.872. The broadest model, using all baseline numeric predictors, achieved AUC 0.911. 
The strongest biomarker model in baseline MCI was led by APOE4 count and the CSF pTau/A beta 42 
ratio, with smaller contributions from plasma pTau217 and CSF tau markers. The MRI plus DWI plus 
memory plus vitals model was dominated by ADNI memory, delayed recall measures, and hippocampal 
volume. In the all-baseline model, the strongest contributors were ADNI memory, hippocampal volume, 
CSF pTau/A beta 42 ratio, CDR-SB, language and executive composites, and delayed recall. 
These MCI-stage results differ from the CN-stage pattern. In baseline MCI, the MRI plus DWI plus 
memory plus vitals panel clearly outperformed the strongest biomarker subset, suggesting that once 
prodromal impairment is present, structural and cognitive measures carry more of the near- to mid-term 
prognostic signal. 
Long-horizon baseline prediction for eventual CN to AD 
The third analysis focused only on baseline CN subjects who eventually reached AD, either directly or 
after first passing through MCI. Among 1,601 baseline CN subjects, 46 reached AD at any observed 
horizon and 39 did so within 12 years, which provided the longest workable horizon in this dataset for a 
direct CN to AD endpoint. 
In this 12-year CN to AD analysis, the strongest biomarker panel achieved AUC 0.926. The MRI plus DWI 
plus memory plus vital-sign panel achieved AUC 0.738. The broadest model, using all baseline numeric 
predictors, achieved AUC 0.940. 
The long-horizon strongest biomarker model was driven mainly by plasma neurofilament light, plasma 
pTau217, the CSF pTau/A beta 42 ratio, CSF tau, plasma A beta 42/A beta 40, and tau PET. In the all-
baseline model, the top contributors were normalized hippocampal volume, plasma pTau217, plasma 
neurofilament light, MOCA, CSF pTau/A beta 42 ratio, hippocampal volume, tau PET, and plasma A beta 
42/A beta 40. 
This third analysis suggests that the long-range signal for eventual AD among baseline CN subjects is not 
dominated by cognition alone. Instead, it appears to depend heavily on a combination of structural 
neurodegeneration and invasive or blood-based molecular markers. However, the event count is small 
enough that this result should be interpreted as promising rather than definitive. 
Interpreting invasive versus non-invasive performance across stages 
Taken together, the timing and prediction results support a two-part story. 
First, invasive markers appear earlier in the transition out of normal cognition. CSF measures reached the 
earliest detectable divergence, and PET also separated future converters from stable controls before 
most non-invasive measures. This supports the view that molecular pathology is detectable before 
downstream structural injury and before clear clinical decline, in line with CSF-first and early-cascade 
studies, while also refining the exact ordering within that early window [1-7,15,16]. 
Second, non-invasive markers become increasingly competitive as the time horizon shortens or as 
disease stage advances. MRI hippocampal atrophy, plasma GFAP , plasma pTau217, memory composites, 

===== PAGE 9 =====
and cognition all contributed materially to prediction. In baseline MCI, the non-invasive-heavy MRI plus 
DWI plus memory plus vitals panel outperformed the strongest biomarker subset. In the separate long-
horizon CN to AD analysis, the strongest biomarker panel remained much stronger than the MRI plus DWI 
plus memory plus vitals subset, while the broad integrated model performed best overall. In other words, 
non-invasive tools may not define the earliest biological onset, but they can still carry substantial 
practical value when combined with molecular markers, which is consistent with recent blood-based 
and multimodal studies but should be interpreted alongside reports showing that blood-marker 
performance can vary substantially by cohort, outcome, and comparator modality [9-33]. 
Discussion 
This study is best understood as a two-stage clinical transition analysis. The first stage addresses early 
deviation from normal cognition, where the present data favor invasive molecular markers, especially 
CSF tau-amyloid imbalance. The corrected subject-level timing analysis placed the CSF pTau/A beta 42 
ratio and CSF A beta 42 at the leading edge of detectable divergence, ahead of most imaging, plasma, 
and cognitive measures. This general pattern is consistent with classic biomarker cascade models and 
especially with work arguing that CSF amyloid abnormalities can be detected before amyloid PET and 
before overt neurodegeneration [1-7,15,16]. 
That result matters because it shifts the narrative away from a simple amyloid-PET-first story. Amyloid 
PET remained early, but after correcting the repeated-measures issue it no longer occupied the singular 
top position. Instead, the earliest nominal signal came from a CSF composite reflecting both amyloid and 
tau biology, and the FDR summary still supported CSF A beta 42 as the most robust earliest signal. At the 
same time, recent blood-based studies caution against overstating a strict invasive-first hierarchy, 
because plasma p-tau217 and combined plasma-marker strategies can sometimes approach CSF or PET 
performance for pathology detection or prediction in selected settings [6,15,16,26,29,30,32]. 
The second stage addresses progression from MCI to AD, where the present work is strongest on 
prediction rather than longitudinal timing. The higher event count in MCI made the 4-year prediction task 
more stable, and the resulting AUC of 0.893 suggests that multimodal baseline information is already 
highly informative once subjects have entered the prodromal stage. This interpretation fits recent work 
showing that stage-aware multimodal models, anatomical staging frameworks, and integrated genetics-
plus-imaging-plus-cognition approaches can substantially improve prediction of MCI conversion [20-25]. 
The non-invasive findings remain important. MRI hippocampal atrophy and plasma GFAP reached 
divergence at 5 years before transition out of normal cognition, and plasma pTau217 emerged at 4 years. 
These are not trivial delays. A 4-year to 5-year lead time is still highly relevant for many screening and trial 
applications, especially given the feasibility advantages of blood tests and MRI compared with lumbar 
puncture or PET. The implication is not that non-invasive markers are inadequate, but that they may 
represent a later and more scalable layer of the detection stack. That interpretation is broadly compatible 
with recent plasma biomarker studies emphasizing early but not necessarily earliest change in plasma p-
tau217, GFAP , and plasma Aβ42/Aβ40, and with longer-horizon studies showing that blood-based 
markers can remain clinically informative even when they do not define the first detectable abnormality 
[8-20]. 

===== PAGE 10 =====
The prediction analyses reinforce that distinction. For near-term 4-year prediction, multivariable models 
performed well in both baseline CN and baseline MCI groups, with especially strong performance in the 
MCI to AD task. For the 7-year comparisons, the broad multimodal baseline model performed best in 
both baseline CN and baseline MCI cohorts, while the intermediate MRI plus DWI plus memory plus 
vitals panel was notably stronger in baseline MCI than in baseline CN. For the 12-year CN to AD analysis, 
the strongest biomarker panel was already highly discriminative, and the broad multimodal model 
performed best overall. This suggests that once multiple domains are integrated, practical prediction 
depends less on a single earliest modality and more on combining complementary markers across 
molecular, structural, and cognitive levels, a conclusion that is well aligned with recent multimodal 
prediction literature [20-25] but also consistent with challenge studies showing that blood markers, 
imaging, and CSF each gain or lose relative advantage depending on the endpoint and cohort under study 
[26-33]. 
A plausible clinical translation is a tiered workflow. A first-pass scalable screen could rely on non-
invasive markers such as plasma assays, MRI, and cognitive composites. Individuals with elevated risk 
could then be escalated to more invasive confirmatory assessment with CSF or PET. The current results 
are consistent with that model because invasive modalities appeared earliest, while non-invasive 
modalities remained sufficiently informative to support broad risk stratification across later clinical 
transition stages. This tiered interpretation is also compatible with recent literature on plasma 
prescreening, multimodal enrichment, and pragmatic trial-selection workflows [6,20,26,29,30,32,33]. 
Limitations 
Several limitations are important. 
First, modality coverage was highly uneven. MRI was relatively dense, while tau PET, CSF , and plasma 
were much sparser. Sparse coverage can change which markers appear earliest because a biomarker 
cannot diverge in a time window where very few samples exist. 
Second, modality alignment relied on exact visit-key matching with nearest-date fallback within 365 
days. This was retained because tighter matching would have removed too much data. The resulting 
paper therefore reflects the best practical analysis under the available data structure, but the temporal 
ordering should still be interpreted with caution. 
Third, most baseline cognitively normal progressors converted to MCI rather than directly to AD. The 
longitudinal timing results therefore mainly represent the biology of transition from normal cognition into 
prodromal impairment rather than direct progression to dementia. 
Fourth, the paper now emphasizes three related analyses, but the longitudinal timing analysis itself was 
only run for the CN-based transition. The MCI stage and the long-horizon CN to AD stage are represented 
here through prediction rather than separate biomarker timing analyses. 
Fifth, the long-horizon CN to AD analysis was based on only 39 events within 12 years and 46 eventual AD 
events overall. Those estimates are therefore especially vulnerable to optimism and sampling instability. 
Sixth, the work is retrospective and limited to ADNI. The resulting timelines and model performance 
estimates should be viewed as internally valid study outputs rather than ready-made clinical thresholds. 

===== PAGE 11 =====
Conclusion 
The final integrated analysis now supports a three-part early-detection story. For the earliest transition 
out of normal cognition, the earliest detectable deviations were invasive molecular markers, led 
nominally by the CSF pTau/A beta 42 ratio and robustly by CSF A beta 42 in the FDR summary. For later 
clinical progression, especially baseline MCI to AD, multimodal baseline prediction was strong. For the 
separate long-horizon baseline CN to AD endpoint, a small but usable set of events suggested that 
plasma and CSF markers plus structural MRI can support meaningful prediction more than a decade 
before first AD diagnosis. 
The practical implication is not that one modality replaces the others. Instead, the data support a layered 
strategy: invasive biomarkers for the earliest biological detection, non-invasive biomarkers for scalable 
longitudinal screening, integrated multimodal models for strong stage-specific prediction, and a 
cautiously interpreted long-horizon CN to AD signal that warrants external validation.
